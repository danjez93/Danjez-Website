Happiness Food

Homepage redesign and conversion optimization for a food products e-commerce — reducing cognitive load in the purchase path and establishing a scalable UI system for the team.

Overview

A homepage that was doing too much — and converting too little

Happiness Food sells natural and organic food products online. Their homepage had grown organically over two years — banners piled on top of banners, multiple competing CTAs, and a product grid that showed everything at once. Conversion rates were flat and bounce rates were high.

My brief was clear: redesign the homepage to focus attention on the purchase path, reduce visual noise, and lay the groundwork for a design system that would keep future additions consistent.

Hypothesis

"Users aren't converting because they can't find a clear path to purchase. Too many options presented equally = no decision made."

01 — Analysis

Reading the data before touching the design

I started by reviewing Hotjar session recordings and heatmaps from 200 sessions on the existing homepage. The data confirmed the hypothesis: users scrolled past the hero banner immediately (low engagement), clicked on product images but abandoned on the product page (a separate problem), and almost never interacted with the secondary navigation categories.

02 — Benchmarking

Learning from food e-commerce that converts

I benchmarked 8 food and CPG e-commerce sites known for strong conversion: iHerb, Thrive Market, Trader Joe's online, and regional competitors. Common patterns in high performers: single hero CTA, category entry points below fold, social proof before product grid.

03 — Redesign

One path, clearly signed

The redesign reduced the above-fold content to three elements: a hero image, a single benefit headline, and one primary CTA. Category navigation moved to a sticky strip below the hero. Social proof (ratings, customer count) was added before the product grid to reduce purchase anxiety.

04 — Design System

Preventing the next round of inconsistency

Alongside the homepage redesign, I documented the UI components as a lightweight system — color tokens, type styles, and a component set for the most reused elements: product cards, buttons, form inputs, and the category strip. This gave the internal team a reference to maintain consistency in future additions.
