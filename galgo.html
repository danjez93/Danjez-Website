Galgo — Credit Access for the Informal Economy

Designing a mobile-first credit product for workers in LATAM who have been systematically excluded from traditional banking — people with real financial needs but no credit history.

Overview

A credit product that meets people where they are

Galgo is a fintech targeting informal workers in LATAM — people who earn money daily but have no payslips, no bank account, and no credit score. Traditional banks call them "non-banked." We called them our users.

My role was to design the full mobile experience: from the onboarding and credit application flow, through the loan management dashboard, to the repayment process. Every decision had to work with low digital literacy, variable connectivity, and high trust risk (first-time credit is a high-stakes moment).

Core Problem

"How do we give someone a first credit product when we have no data on them — and they've never used a financial app before?"

01 — Discovery & Research

Understanding the informal worker's financial life

We conducted 12 contextual interviews with informal workers (market vendors, taxi drivers, domestic workers) in Bogotá and Medellín. The goal was to understand how they currently manage debt, savings, and financial risk — not to validate our assumptions, but to challenge them.

Key finding: informal credit networks (prestamistas, natilleras) are built entirely on social trust and verbal contracts. Digital products that feel cold, bureaucratic, or opaque are immediately distrusted.

02 — Benchmarking

How informal credit works today

Before designing anything, we mapped the existing mental models: the informal lender (prestamista), the rotating savings group (natillera), and the neighborhood credit store. Each had a distinct interaction pattern we needed to reference — not copy.

03 — UX Design

Designing for trust, not features

The credit application flow had to do two things simultaneously: collect enough data to make a credit decision, and make the user feel safe enough to provide it. Every screen had to earn trust before asking for the next piece of information.

I designed the flow as a progressive disclosure sequence — starting with the least sensitive data (phone number) and building toward more sensitive inputs (income, purpose of loan) only after establishing clarity about how data is used.

04 — UI Design

A visual language built on warmth and clarity

The UI needed to feel warm, not clinical — approachable enough for first-time app users, but professional enough to convey financial credibility. We used high-contrast typography, generous tap targets (48px minimum), and clear feedback states at every step.
